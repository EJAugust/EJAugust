An Integrated Development Environment (IDE) is an app that provides tools to streamline the development of other apps.

Last week, I published a note about the multiverse metaphor for software engineering. This is a metaphor that explains the difference between explains the difference between using an app and editing the app live.

In case you didn't see it, here's a refresher:

But what do we call an IDE that has everything you need to modify its own code while it's still running? Let's call it: a Universal Integrated Development Environment (UIDE) because it can, in theory, be transformed into whatever you need it to be.

A UIDE is an application that lets you explore the multiverse like a starship captain. Pretty cool, right?

Adding Gems Doesn't Make It Fun

Increasingly, designers are seeing the need for gameplay features in order to add intrinsic value to their applications.

Many serious applications (including workplace applications) now include fictional currency (You just got 10 Gems!, Invite a friend to earn 5 Coins), badges (Top Contributor, 5 Day Streak!) and confetti every time you ‚Äúsubmit‚Äù.

Maybe it's just me, but those things aren't enough to make an app fun all by themselves. They've been used as a bandaid for tedious (and at times, downright exploitative) user experiences, so I've already learned to associate them with tedium.

My Dream GUIDE

Those things aren't what I crave in a game. It's the story, the characters, the exploration, the creativity, and perhaps even some crazy physics.

That's what gets my blood pumping.

That's what drew me to the sci-fi classics ‚Äî Star Trek, Stargate, Star Wars, etc. and that's also what drew me to writing this note.

Imagine a game that wasn't trying to cover up that it's secretly an IDE as much as it was trying to just be a game.

But this game lets the community create and play mods for it, from within the game, without having to close the game.

This concept is a bit like a desktop operating system. You can change the behavior of it by adding, modifying and removing system files. You can often see these changes reflected even without shutting the computer down.

I get an unusual amount of joy from playing around with vintage operating systems. For me, they already are a kind of game. But I'm willing to admit that this isn't everybody's cup of tea. By gamifying the operating system, we can nurture a diverse group of contributors who will take the game to the next level.

Next-Level Sandbox Game

There are already some fantastic precedents for turning art, architecture, design and engineering into an engaging and collaborative gameplay experience - exactly what I want my GUIDE to be able to do.

One of my favorite examples of this is Minecraft. In that game, players get to break their world into small parts and reassemble them however they want. Redstone allows players to create circuits and machines which can automate complex tasks.

Fans of Minecraft have done some amazing things with it. A quick search on YouTube will show you mind-boggling creations, including low-level engineering (like making working computers).

Sandbox games are fun, but you can't edit the source code of the Minecraft executable by placing redstone. As a software architect, what I really want is a game that directly maps its fictional technology and narrative structure to the game's real source code and development methodology - and then lets me engage with those things at native speed.

Dare I say‚Ä¶ warp speed.

A Familiar Danger

A good game usually has at least a little bit of danger. In Minecraft, depending on the difficulty, hostile mobs will spawn, attack the player and destroy their hard work.

In general, desktop computer users already know that they shouldn't modify and delete system files unless they know what they're doing. The machine might hang forever, crash, get stuck in a boot loop, or even become a brick.

We could add enemies into the GUIDE, but we don't actually need to. Changing the code of an app while it's still running is like a surgeon operating on themself. The GUIDE is not in danger. It is the danger.

(Thankfully, its just a game.)

Back to the Multiverse

In addition to helping users understand the two kinds of change that can take place in the app, the multiverse metaphor also helps because:

  1. It's a compelling tie-in to a fictional narrative - more so than file folders.

  2. The risks and rewards are baked into our cultural consciousness.

  3. Its an all encompassing metaphor that is just begging to have all of its extensions mapped out.

There aren't a lot of movies about the risks of importing the wrong third-party SDK into a git repository. There are a lot of movies about playing around with the universe and the dangers of toying with science and technology.

The Growth Never Ends

One of the most important aspects to making a game fun is about balancing its pacing. The difficulty has to ramp up to meet the player's performance. The same is true about education. In fact, that's what a challenging, well-paced game is doing - it's teaching gameplay mechanics to players who started completely from scratch.

In the GUIDE, those mechanics are software engineering mechanics.

The ideal narrative is one that provides players with a ladder - a journey that lets them advance one step at a time from newbie to journeyman to power user to someone that can completely transform their world.

The Challenge

It may require a little inspiration (and a lot of math) but I believe that creating such a GUIDE is both doable and worth doing.

The biggest challenges of this project are in creating a framework that can match 1:1 all of its actual software components with in-game fictional components and preventing malicious misuse.

For the last three years, I've been researching and developing a quantitative framework that I intend to accomplish exactly this. But it's not easy, and it takes time.

The Power of Continuous Improvement

I'll admit, this doesn't look like much, but it might be enough to get the ball rolling.

After all, the whole idea behind a UIDE is that we can instantly start trial-and-error development with it. Just as soon as we have version 1.0.0, we can rapidly prototype version 2.0.0 without closing version 1.0.0.

It will crash. Work will get lost, especially in the early days. The entire code base may need to be refactored again and again before our UIDE becomes a bona fide (fun) GUIDE.

They say the sky is the limit. But with a GUIDE, if you don't like the sky, you can replace it with something else.
